An IFRA Certificate of Conformity is a statement issued by the supplier of a fragrance mixture for a defined intended use. It is not an “IFRA approval,” it is not issued by IFRA, and it does not replace a cosmetic safety assessment or destination-market compliance review.

What buyers should verify on the document

Match the fragrance reference on the certificate to the material used in the formula. Check the issuer, document date or amendment basis, intended product category and maximum permitted use level where stated. A certificate prepared for one application cannot simply be reused for every product format.

The finished perfume concentration must remain within the applicable limit for its category. If the fragrance reference, dosage or product use changes, request a current document rather than assuming the previous certificate still applies.

What the certificate does not prove

It does not certify the perfume factory, approve the finished product, establish that every ingredient is legal in the destination market or confirm that the label is correct. It also does not replace toxicological assessment, allergen review, stability work or other documents required by the responsible party.

How it fits into a product file

The IFRA document normally sits alongside the fragrance specification, SDS or safety information, formula records and other evidence used by the brand's regulatory reviewer. The correct file depends on the market. In the EU, for example, the Responsible Person and safety assessor have obligations beyond collecting an IFRA certificate.

Ask a manufacturer which documents can be supplied and at what project stage. Some information is tied to the approved formula and therefore cannot be finalized while the scent and concentration are still changing.

Official reference: IFRA guidance on Certificates of Conformity.
